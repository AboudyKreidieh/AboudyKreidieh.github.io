About Me

My name is Abdul Rahman (Aboudy) Kreidieh. I am a fourth year PhD candidate at the University of California, Berkeley, currently advised by Alexandre M. Bayen. I am a member of Berkeley Artificial Intelligence Research (BAIR), Berkeley Deep Drive (BDD), and the Congestion Impacts Reduction via CAV-in-the-loop Lagrangian Energy Smoothing (CIRCLES) project, and have in the past served roles in the RISE Lab, California PATH, Toyota InfoTech Labs, and Nissan Alliance SV Lab.

I am interested in exploring the impacts of existing and upcoming mixed human/automated systems, and designing tools that enhance the roles they serve in society. This, I believe, is becoming increasingly relevant in recent years, with devices such as self-driving cars and automated recommendation systems looking as though they will serve a more pervasive role in our everyday lives. During my PhD, I have studied the role of mixed-autonomy systems in the context of traffic control via automated vehicles in particular. Through my work, I aim to demonstrate that expertly designed automated driving systems, powered in part by recent innovations in machine learning and artificial intelligence, can greatly improve the energy efficiency, throughput, and overall human experience of driving in a variety of congested traffic settings.

In my free time, you can find me catching the latest Liverpool game, binging as much content as I can handle, and, you know, stuff that sounds cool to you too.

Flow


Figure 1. Sample AV control problem explored in Flow

Flow is an open-source framework developed at UC Berkeley that aims to enable investigations on the applicability of reinforcement learning (RL) to the field of intelligent transportation studies. It combines state-of-the-art traffic microsimulators (sumo and Aimsum) with several popular RL libraries (RLlib, rllab, and stable-baselines, among other) to test various RL algorithms and design control paradigms for traffic management problems in simulation.
I have been fortunate enough to serve a central role in the development of Flow from its onset. Since then, I have contributed to the decision-making and implemention of many of the features that have allowed Flow to flexibly support a variety of traffic control problems while also being computationally efficient enough to handle sample-intensive RL algorithms. Today, the Flow community is fastly growing and is used by dozens of research groups and companies alike. To learn more or try out Flow for yourself, feel free to explore the links below!

CIRCLES

The CIRCLES consortium is an interdisciplinary group established in 2020 and currently funded by the DOE. This project has brought together experts from leading universities OpenAI's in fields ranging from transportation, machine learning, control theory, and cyber-physical systems with the hopes of demonstrating that automated vehicles at even low penetration rates can improve the global energy-efficiency of vehicles in typical congested highways.
During my PhD, I have worked alongside CIRCLES on a number of fronts. This has included designing the framework used to simulate real-world traffic jams, and validating the performance of various controllers within this simulation (an example of which can be seen in Figure 2). Currently, I am looking to couple a number of these control strategies with modern techniques in reinforcement and imitation learning to produce behaviors that are robust to anticipated real-world limitations in communication, sensing, and control. Hierarchical RL

Hierarchical models for deep neural networks may serve a pivotal role in a variety of relevant reinforcement learning (RL) problems. Similar to convolutional models in computer vision, and recurrents models in natural language processing, hierarchical models exploit the natural (temporal) structure of RL problems. This greatly benefits learning when feedback is sparsely defined or when success can only be achieved by following unique behaviors across long periods of time. This benefit, I believe, is of particular interest in traffic control problems, as the properties of traffic congestion propagate slowly through a network, and as such require long-term reasoning to understand and solve. As a result, I have decidated a significant portion of PhD studies tring to design models and optimization procedures that adapt hierarchical RL algorithms to the field of mixed-autonomy traffic.
